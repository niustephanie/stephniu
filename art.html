Roses in the Archive

This collage pays tribute to the Samsui women, Singaporean women from the Samsui province in south China, and particularly their contributions to Christmas Island. I first learned about the Samsui women through an article by Christopher Su in The Islander, Christmas Island’s community newsletter, which notes:

There are dozens of pages documenting Asian females as prostitutes in the local history books with the odd mention of them as ‘amah’ nannies to British children. Not one page is dedicated to the achievements of Asian women or recognition of their labour.

The three photographs in this collage are the only known images to exist that depict the Samsui women on Christmas Island; all three show the women at work. What little we know about the women comes from state and national archives: they did hard manual labor. They wore distinctive red headscarves with a boxy silhouette. They came from the Samsui district. In my desire to imagine the Samsui women as more than just workers, as whole people with rich interior lives, I wrote a poem in Mandarin from the perspective of a Samsui woman writing home from Christmas Island.

The red dotted lines trace the shape of the modern-day Samsui / Sanshui district based on Google Maps. Text appearing in white boxes is quoted from Jan Adams and Marg Neales “Christmas Island – The Early Years” (1993), and The National Library of Singapore’s “Samsui women” by Koh, Qi Rui Vincent (December 2014).
Left and right photos: Samsui women at the wharf construction site of Christmas Island, National Archives of Australia - www.naa.gov.au Center photo: from Jan Adams and Marg Neales “Christmas Island – The Early Years” (1993).
